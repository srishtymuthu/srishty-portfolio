01 — Research Context

The invisible cost of always having an answer.

ChatGPT and other generative AI tools arrived in higher education almost overnight, and adoption among students was immediate and widespread. But as these tools replaced the friction of not-knowing — the "productive struggle" that prompts students to ask peers, visit office hours, or work through problems together — something changed in how students related to each other academically.

This study asked: what happens to student learning communities when AI becomes the default first resource? The question sits at the intersection of educational technology, social dynamics, and the design of AI systems — and the answer has implications for how we build and deploy AI tools in academic contexts.

02 — Research Methods

A mixed-methods approach to a behavioral question.

Understanding shifts in social behavior requires getting close to experience — numbers alone can't capture how a student feels when they realize they haven't talked to a classmate about coursework in weeks. We designed a mixed-methods study combining quantitative survey data with qualitative depth.

Quantitative

Survey Design & Statistical Analysis

Designed survey instruments to measure frequency of AI use, frequency of peer interaction for academic help, and self-reported changes in learning habits. Statistical comparisons across demographics and AI usage levels.

Qualitative

Semi-Structured Interviews

In-depth interviews with students about their actual behaviors, attitudes toward AI tools, and perceived changes in their academic social lives. Designed to surface experiences that surveys can't capture.

Synthesis

Thematic Analysis & Coding

Systematic qualitative coding of interview transcripts to identify recurring themes. Findings triangulated against survey data to identify patterns that held across both data sources.

My contributions spanned end-to-end research operations: instrument design, participant outreach, data collection, cleaning and analysis pipelines, and translating findings into publication-ready outputs aligned with ACM formatting and peer review standards.

03 — Key Findings

When the chatbot replaces the study group.

  • Displacement of peer interaction: Students reported turning to ChatGPT before — or instead of — asking classmates or TAs, reducing the frequency of collaborative problem-solving that normally builds academic community.
  • Erosion of learning communities: Study groups became less common as students found AI-assisted solo work more efficient. Convenience traded away the social scaffolding that supports deeper learning.
  • Epistemic changes: Students described changes in how they processed difficulty — the instinct to "sit with" a hard problem was being replaced by the instinct to immediately offload it to AI.
  • Awareness without behavior change: Many students recognized that their AI use might be affecting their learning but felt unable to or uninterested in changing — raising design questions about how these tools surface their own effects.

"All roads lead to ChatGPT."

— Study participant, characterizing how every academic question eventually ended up in the chatbox

04 — Design & Policy Implications

What this means for how we build and teach with AI.

The findings surfaced questions that extend beyond individual student behavior into the design of AI tools and educational policy. If AI is eroding the social infrastructure of learning, that's partly a design problem — one that tool builders have some responsibility to address.

Research-to-design bridge.

This work directly informs questions like: should AI tutoring tools actively prompt students to ask a peer before giving an answer? Should LMS integrations surface AI usage patterns to instructors? How do we design AI that supports rather than supplants the human relationships that make learning stick? These are open problems — and the value of this research is in making them concrete enough to act on.
