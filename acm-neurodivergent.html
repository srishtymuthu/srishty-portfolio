01 — Research Context

Active learning assumes a kind of mind it wasn't designed around.

Active and collaborative learning — group work, pair programming, think-pair-share, live discussion — has become a cornerstone of modern CS education. Research shows it improves outcomes for the average student. But average is doing a lot of work in that sentence.

Neurodivergent students — those with ADHD, autism, dyslexia, sensory processing differences, and other cognitive profiles — often experience the same pedagogical structures very differently. The implicit social contracts of group work, the sensory demands of collaborative spaces, the expectation of spontaneous verbal participation: these can create friction that makes learning harder, not easier.

This study set out to understand those experiences directly, in the students' own words — and to surface actionable implications for how we design CS classrooms and learning tools.

02 — Why This Research

HCI as an advocate for underheard experiences.

Much of the research on active learning centers on aggregate outcomes — average test scores, average engagement, average retention. Neurodivergent students are often a statistical minority whose experiences are averaged away. But designing systems around averages means designing systems that fail non-average people.

"I can't read your mind."

— Study participant, describing the difficulty of navigating unspoken social norms in group work

This quote — which became the paper's title — captures something important: collaborative learning requires a fluency in implicit social communication that not everyone shares equally. When the curriculum assumes that fluency, it excludes.

03 — Research Methods

Listening carefully to lived experience.

Qualitative research is the right tool when you're trying to understand the texture of experience — not just what happens, but what it feels like, and why. We used semi-structured interviews to give participants space to describe their experiences in their own terms, with follow-up probes to go deeper on themes as they emerged.

01

Participant recruitment & sampling

Recruited neurodivergent computing students through intentional sampling — ensuring a range of neurodivergent profiles, course levels, and active learning contexts. Participants self-identified and consented to discuss their experiences.

02

Interview guide design

Developed a semi-structured protocol covering: experiences with group work and pair programming, specific challenges in active learning formats, coping strategies, and suggestions for what would make these environments more accessible. Open questions first, more specific probes as needed.

03

Data collection & transcription

Conducted and transcribed interviews, then prepared them for systematic analysis. Careful attention to consent, anonymization, and participant comfort throughout — particularly important given the personal and sometimes sensitive nature of the topics discussed.

04

Thematic analysis & coding

Applied systematic qualitative coding to identify recurring themes across interviews. Used iterative codebook refinement across the research team to ensure reliability. Themes were then organized into a coherent analytical framework for the paper.

04 — Findings & Implications

What the data surfaces about CS education design.

The study's findings are pending final publication (ACM ITiCSE '26). The core contributions center on documenting specific, named friction points that neurodivergent students encounter in active learning contexts, and translating those into design and pedagogical recommendations for CS instructors and EdTech builders.

  • Implicit social norms in group work create disproportionate cognitive load for neurodivergent students — who must simultaneously process content and decode unstated social expectations.
  • Spontaneous participation requirements (cold-calling, live think-alouds) disadvantage students who process more slowly or need preparation time to communicate effectively.
  • Sensory and environmental factors in collaborative classrooms — noise levels, seating arrangements, lighting — affect focus and participation in ways that instructors rarely account for.
  • Individual coping strategies are extensive and effortful — suggesting that students are doing significant invisible work to participate in environments not designed for them.

Toward more inclusive CS pedagogy.

This work contributes to a growing body of research on universal design for learning (UDL) in computing education. Implications span instructor practice (providing advance notice of participation requirements, offering multiple modes of contribution) and tool design (async collaboration features, explicit social scaffolding in EdTech platforms). The goal is classrooms and tools that don't require neurodivergent students to mask in order to participate.
