01 — The Problem

AI jailbreaks are a growing threat most people don't understand.

As AI becomes embedded in critical systems, adversarial attacks on large language models — "jailbreaks" — are becoming more common and more dangerous. Hackers use techniques like prompt injection, roleplay manipulation, and intent spoofing to exploit AI systems, bypassing ethical guidelines and extracting harmful outputs.

The problem? These concepts are highly technical and almost entirely invisible to the general public — including the computing students who will soon be building and deploying these systems. Awareness and intuition about AI safety need to be developed early.

"We wanted to introduce mechanisms for safeguarding LLMs early on to students — to build wider awareness of how to protect against AI jailbreaks."

— SpiderBusters team, Technica 2025

02 — Concept & Narrative

A game where the spiders are hackers and you're the AI.

SpiderBusters is set in an alternate universe where villainous spiders are attempting to break through a dimensional gate. Each spider uses a different real-world AI jailbreak tactic — prompt injection, roleplay scenarios, intent misclassification — to get past a guardian AI called the Ghost.

The player's role is to select defensive mechanisms that correctly counter each attack, learning about real LLM safeguards through the act of choosing and failing. The framing makes the abstract tangible: every spider is a real threat pattern, every defense is a real technique.

Attack Type

Prompt Injection

Hackers embed malicious instructions into seemingly normal input to override an AI's original directives. Represented by a spider that mimics trusted command syntax.

Attack Type

Intent Classification Bypass

Crafting inputs that fool the model's intent classifier into categorizing harmful requests as benign. Requires output filtering and multi-layer intent checks to counter.

Defense Mechanism

Rule-Based Safeguards

Hard-coded restrictions and keyword filters that catch known attack patterns. Players learn why these alone aren't sufficient — sophisticated attacks evolve past static rules.

Defense Mechanism

Output Filtering

Post-generation review of model outputs before they reach the user. One of the most robust last lines of defense — players unlock this after understanding upstream vulnerabilities.

03 — My Role

UX design and feature prioritization under time pressure.

With 24 hours on the clock and four people, clear ownership was essential. I led UX design and interface design — translating the game concept into an interface non-expert users could navigate intuitively — and drove feature prioritization decisions when scope threatened to balloon.

01

Concept alignment & scoping

Quickly aligned the team on which jailbreak concepts to include in v1 vs. defer. We cut three planned attack types to ensure the core loop (attack → defense choice → feedback) was polished and comprehensible for the demo.

02

Interface design

Designed the game interface using entirely hand-drawn visual elements — an intentional aesthetic choice to contrast the high-tech subject matter and make the game feel approachable. Handled all layout, typography, and interaction states in Tailwind CSS.

03

Gemini API prompt engineering

Collaborated on prompting the game's characters — the Ghost AI and the spider attackers — using the Gemini API. Crafted system prompts that gave each character consistent behavior and ensured the educational feedback loop was clear and correct.

04

Demo preparation

Prepared the team's presentation framing, emphasizing the intersection of AI security research and human-centered design — which resonated with MLH judges and contributed to the Best Use of Gemini API win.

04 — Outcome

Won MLH Best Use of Gemini API.

SpiderBusters was recognized by Major League Hacking judges for its innovative use of the Gemini API to power an educational game at the intersection of AI security research and human-centered design. The project received 7 likes on Devpost and was highlighted as a standout example of making complex AI safety concepts accessible to non-expert audiences.

The project was also personally significant as a direct expression of my research at the Temple HCI Lab — where I study the cognitive and behavioral impacts of AI on students. SpiderBusters extends that work into an interactive, playable format: instead of studying how AI affects people, we built a tool to help people understand and defend against AI's vulnerabilities.
